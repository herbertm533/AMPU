# Historical Context: Deaths & Retirements (Phase 2.4.1)

## Era window
1772 — present. The feature spans the entire game timeline. Mortality and
retirement behavior differ structurally across at least five sub-eras:
colonial/founding (1772–1820), antebellum (1820–1865), Gilded Age (1865–1900),
20th century pension era (1900–1965), modern era (1965–present).

## Binding facts (PM should treat these as ground truth)

**Conditional life expectancy (life expectancy *at age 20*, not at birth) is the
right anchor for politicians.** Politicians enter elected office mid-20s
typically; almost none died in infancy. The standard "1700s life expectancy
was 36" figure is life expectancy at birth, dragged down by infant mortality.
A 20-year-old in 1772 colonial America who survived childhood could
reasonably expect to live into his late 50s or 60s. [Consensus history]

- For Princeton graduates 1709–1819, e₂₀ ≈ 36.2 years (i.e., expected death age
  ~56). For Maryland legislators born 1750–1764, e₂₀ ≈ 34.7 years (~55). For
  South Carolina legislators born 1750–1764, e₂₀ ≈ 31.7 years (~52 — the
  malarial South was notably deadlier). Yale graduates 1785–1814: e₂₀ ≈ 40.4
  years (~60). [Consensus, well-documented]
- 1850 e₀ (life expectancy at birth, white males) ≈ 40.4 years; e₀ stayed
  roughly flat at 38–44 years through 1880. From 1880 to 1900, e₀ rose from
  ~39 to ~47.8 years (mostly via reduced child mortality, not adult longevity
  gains). [Consensus — Haines decennial life tables]
- 1900 (CDC/NCHS): e₀ ≈ 47.3; e₆₅ ≈ 11.9 years for both sexes (males 11.5,
  females 12.2). [Consensus]
- 1950 (CDC/NCHS): e₀ ≈ 68.2; e₆₅ ≈ 13.9 years (males 12.8, females 15.0).
  [Consensus]
- 1970: e₆₅ ≈ 15.2 years (males 13.1, females 17.0). [Consensus]
- Recent (2023): for a 20-year-old male, ~56.7 more years of expected life
  (death ~age 77). [Consensus]

**A 60-year-old in 1900 could expect ~14 more years of life.** Improvements
at ages 60+ between 1900 and 2001 were ~55% reduction in mortality
probability for males, ~65% for females. The bulk of 20th-century life
expectancy gains came from reduced infant/child mortality, not from
adult-age extension; at age 60+, the absolute gains have been more modest.
[Consensus — SSA actuarial Study 120, "Mortality Trends in the United
States, 1900–60"]

## Key figures
Beyond the death-age table above, the figures the PM should sanity-check
mechanics against:
- **William Henry Harrison** (d. 1841, age 68): died of pneumonia (or, per
  recent scholarship, septic shock from contaminated White House water) 31
  days into his presidency. The canonical "Frail at oldest age band" outcome.
- **Woodrow Wilson** (incapacitated by stroke Oct 2, 1919, at age 62): hidden
  by his wife and physician; remained nominally president 17 more months.
  Classic "should have retired" but politically did not.
- **FDR** (d. April 12, 1945, age 63): polio from age 39, declining health
  through 1944 reelection, died in office of cerebral hemorrhage 82 days into
  his 4th term. Frail by any reasonable definition; served until death.
- **John Quincy Adams** (d. 1848, age 80, on the House floor): after losing
  the presidency 1828, served 17 years in the House until his death — the
  archetypal "served until death" pattern.

## Timeline of relevant events
- **1796**: Washington publishes Farewell Address; declines third term at age
  64. Establishes the **two-term tradition** — the first major precedent for
  voluntary political retirement in the US. [Consensus — Mount Vernon]
- **1804** (Jul 11): Hamilton-Burr duel; Hamilton dies next day at 47. Dueling
  was a politician occupational risk through ~1840 (Jackson dueled; Clay shot
  Randolph's coat; in 1826 the Secretary of State, 5 Congressmen, and an Army
  general dueled). [User binding: out of scope for v1.]
- **1841** (Apr 4): William Henry Harrison dies, first president to die in
  office. Establishes the practical relevance of presidential succession.
- **1848** (Feb 23): J.Q. Adams collapses on House floor, dies two days later.
- **1852**: Clay (June) and Webster (Oct) both die in office. Marks the end
  of the antebellum "great triumvirate" generation.
- **1865** (Apr 14): Lincoln assassination. Plus Seward survives separate
  attack same night.
- **1881**: Garfield shot (dies September, age 49).
- **1889**: Bismarck's German pension system establishes a state-level
  retirement age (age 70, lowered to 65 in 1916, after Bismarck's death).
  NOT the source of US "65" age. [Consensus — SSA history]
- **1901**: McKinley assassinated.
- **1919** (Oct): Wilson stroke.
- **1935** (Aug 14): Social Security Act signed. Age 65 chosen pragmatically
  from existing state pension systems and Railroad Retirement (about half
  used 65, half used 70). This is when "65 = retirement" became a US norm,
  *not* before. [Consensus — SSA, Minneapolis Fed]
- **1945** (Apr 12): FDR dies in office.
- **1951**: 22nd Amendment ratified (two-term limit codified — 155 years
  after Washington's precedent).
- **1963** (Nov 22): JFK assassination.
- **1967** (Feb 10): 25th Amendment (presidential succession/incapacity)
  ratified — direct response to JFK assassination and prior gaps (Garfield,
  McKinley, Wilson, Eisenhower heart attack 1955).

## Era-appropriate retirement patterns

**1772–1800 (colonial/founding)**: Voluntary "retirement from public life"
was a republican virtue trope (Cincinnatus model) — Washington's 1783
return-to-Mount-Vernon and 1797 farewell were *deliberate*, exemplary
performances of this ideal. In practice, most figures continued in some
public role until death or political defeat. [Consensus]

**19th century broadly**: Congressional careers were *short* — average
Senator tenure 3–5 years; House 2–3 years; high voluntary non-return rates
(approaching one-third of members not seeking re-election in many sessions;
peak 76% turnover in 1842). But this was largely "going home to state
politics or law practice" voluntary departure — not retirement in the modern
sense of "stop working." [Consensus — CRS R41545, Congress.gov]

**Death-in-office was meaningfully more common pre-1972**: per UMN
Smart Politics analysis, "nearly four times as many U.S. Senators died per
year from 1789 through 1972 than during the last half-century." Total
Senate deaths-in-office to date: 302. [Contested numerator precision; trend
direction consensus.]

**20th century**: Average Senate tenure rose from ~5 years in 1880s to ~11
years today. House tenure rose similarly from ~2–3 to ~8.6 years. This
reflects (a) longer life expectancy, (b) careerist professionalization of
Congress, (c) post-1935 pension/Social Security context making "retirement"
a coherent life stage rather than career exit to law practice. [Consensus —
CRS R41545]

**Modern (post-1980)**: Senate voluntary retirements typically clustered
late 60s through mid-70s. Outliers (Thurmond 100, Byrd 92 in office,
Feinstein 90 in office) demonstrate that institutional incentives reward
late-life service. Today's typical "voluntary retirement age" for senators
is roughly 70–80, with significant deaths-in-office at 80+. [Inference from
published data]

## The Frail trait — historical signal
**Yes, weak but real signal.** Specific cases support a Frail-accelerates-
death mapping:
- W.H. Harrison: died 31 days into presidency at 68 from pneumonia/sepsis.
- FDR: polio paraplegic from 39; visibly declining 1944–45; died at 63.
- Wilson: massive stroke at 62, lingered 5 years dependent on his wife.
- Calhoun (d. 1850, age 68): tuberculosis throughout last decade.
- Webster (d. 1852, age 70): cirrhosis, in office.
The *historical signal is anecdotal*, not statistical; the sample of US
politicians with verifiable "frail" health markers who died early is real
but small. [Inferred — consensus on individual cases, no academic study
ranking US politicians by frailty correlated with mortality]

## The Crisis Manager trait — historical signal
**Weak / mixed.** Lincoln survived the Civil War but was assassinated 1865
(age 56). Washington survived the Revolution and lived to 67. FDR managed
the Depression and WWII but died at 63 (4th term). LBJ managed Civil
Rights / Vietnam and died at 64 (left office voluntarily 1968). Truman
lived to 88. Eisenhower (D-Day) to 78. The sample is too small and the
selection effects too strong (politicians who survived to be crisis
managers were already survivors) to support a strong empirical claim that
crisis management *causes* longevity. This is properly a **game-balance
choice**, not a historically-grounded claim. The user's framing of "Crisis
Manager reduces death rate" is defensible as gameplay, NOT as history.
[Inferred — explicit caveat]

## Period-specific terminology
- "Retired" (1772–1820): meant "withdrew to private estate / farming"
  (Cincinnatus motif). Not pension-funded; mostly self-funded gentry.
- "Retired" (1820–1900): typically meant "did not seek re-election";
  most ex-officeholders returned to law practice, business, or state
  politics rather than full withdrawal from public life.
- "Retired" (post-1935): the modern Social-Security-funded life stage. Use
  this framing only for post-1935 gameplay.
- "65 is retirement age" is a **post-1935 US framing**. For pre-1935 eras,
  the implied retirement age was effectively "when you can no longer
  physically serve, which is 70+." For pre-Civil War eras, "until you die
  or lose an election." [Consensus]

## Common pop-history simplifications and what they get wrong
- "People only lived to ~40 in 1800." False — life expectancy at *birth* was
  ~36–40, but a 20-year-old could expect another 35–40 years. Use e₂₀, not
  e₀, when modeling politicians.
- "Modern medicine doubled lifespans." Partial truth — modern medicine
  dramatically reduced infant/child mortality and 20th-century infectious
  disease deaths. Adult-age longevity gains at 60+ are real but modest
  compared to the e₀ headline number. A 70-year-old in 1900 vs 2000 gained
  ~5–7 years of remaining life, not 30.
- "Washington was elderly when he retired at 64." By modern standards, no.
  By 1796 norms, he was old (e₂₀ for his cohort was mid-50s); his survival
  to 67 was unremarkable but not premature retirement.
- "Founders all died young." False; Franklin 84, Adams 90, Jefferson 83,
  Madison 85. Founders who avoided duels and war disproportionately reached
  their 80s — selection effect of having survived to become a Founder.

## Anachronism watch
- **Current 2.4.1 brackets** (`>= 80: 18%`, `>= 70: 7%`, `>= 60: 2.5%`,
  `< 60: 0.5%`) are roughly modern (post-1980) US population rates and are
  **too low for colonial / antebellum eras at every band**. A 60-year-old
  in 1772 had a meaningfully higher annual death probability than a 60-year-
  old in 2026; same for 70 and 80. Order-of-magnitude rule of thumb: roughly
  1.5–2× modern mortality for ages 60+ in 1772–1860; ~1.2–1.5× for 1860–1900;
  ~1.1× for 1900–1950; baseline for 1950–present. [Inferred from Haines
  e₂₀ and SSA Mortality Trends — these multipliers are illustrative, not
  precise actuarial values.]
- **1772 starting roster includes politicians up to ~70**. Franklin was 66
  in 1772, lived to 84 (1790). Most of the Founding generation in their 60s
  in 1772 actually lived into the 1810s–1820s. The PM should not over-apply
  the higher colonial mortality multiplier to a tail that historically beat
  the odds — the *surviving* founders were a selected population.
- **Retirement gate (`if (p.currentOffice)`)**: historically, only sitting
  officeholders had a "retirement" event meaningful enough to log. Free
  agents (out-of-office politicians waiting for the next race) didn't
  "retire" in any visible sense — they simply stopped running. Lifting this
  gate means modeling "the free-agent gracefully exits the pool" which is
  fine but is a model abstraction, not a historical event type.
- **Pre-1796 "retirement" framing** in UI/log text should respect the
  Cincinnatus norm — "withdraws to private life" reads era-appropriately;
  "retires" reads modern. Post-1935 "retires" reads natural.
- **Out of scope per user binding: dueling**. Note for the record that
  dueling killed Hamilton (1804) and was a real occupational mortality
  source for politicians 1790–1840 (Andrew Jackson dueled multiple times;
  Clay shot Randolph's coat 1826). Omitting it slightly under-models
  founding-era political mortality — acceptable v1 trade.
