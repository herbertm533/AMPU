## Era summaries

### 1772 — Founding / Federalist era (game window c.1772–1820)

How candidates actually won elections in this window:

- **Suffrage was narrow and propertied.** Every colony/state required voters to
  own land, own personal property of a stated value, or pay taxes. Adams
  himself defended this with the view that propertyless men "vote as they are
  directed by some man of property." (C) Estimates of the white-male eligible
  electorate in colonial elections cluster around 50–80% in some New England
  jurisdictions and as low as ~10–25% in some Southern counties; you should
  treat the period as **deference-politics with a narrow electorate**, not a
  mass democracy.
- **Stump speeches existed, but the term and practice were less developed.**
  The "stump speech" term derives from this period (candidates literally
  standing on tree stumps); Patrick Henry was one of the most prominent
  practitioners, having learned a Great-Awakening-derived style of speaking
  "in the people's language and to their hearts." (C)
- **The PRESS was the dominant national-scale persuasion medium.** Pamphlets,
  not stump tours, drove most large-scale political persuasion. The
  best-known examples bracket the era:
  - Thomas Paine's *Common Sense* (Jan 1776) sold ~120,000 copies in three
    months and ~500,000 by war's end in a population of about three million —
    an estimated 20% of colonists owned a copy. (C)
  - The Federalist Papers (Oct 1787 – Apr 1788), 85 essays signed "Publius,"
    were a NEW YORK newspaper ratification campaign, then collected and
    distributed to other states. (C)
- **Backroom politics was central to elective offices that weren't popularly
  contested.** Senators were chosen by state legislatures from 1789 until the
  17th Amendment (ratified 1913). (C) The 1800 House contingent election
  (Jefferson v. Burr) was a backroom contest decided by Federalist
  maneuvering, including Hamilton's intervention against Burr. (C)
- **Sex scandals could be career-limiting but not necessarily fatal.** The
  Hamilton-Reynolds affair (the affair ran 1791–92, the public *Reynolds
  Pamphlet* came in 1797) is the canonical case: Hamilton retained influence
  but the scandal "permanently ended any hope he might have had of becoming
  president." (C, per History.com / Smithsonian)
- **Dueling was acceptable, even compulsory in some circles.** Andrew Jackson
  had killed Charles Dickinson in an 1806 duel, and his violent and dueling
  reputation was an attack vector in 1828 but not disqualifying. (C)

### 1856 — Antebellum / Second Party System collapse (game window c.1856–1880)

How candidates won elections in this window:

- **The 1840 William Henry Harrison "log cabin and hard cider" campaign is the
  watershed — it is widely called "the first modern election campaign."** (C)
  Whig rallies centered on log cabins and free hard cider; iconography
  saturated material culture; slogans like "Tippecanoe and Tyler too" were
  campaign-shaping. By the 1856–1860 window this style was normal.
- **Candidates themselves still did not actively campaign**, with one major
  exception. Stephen A. Douglas in 1860 was "the first Presidential candidate
  to go on the stump" personally. (C) Lincoln, by contrast, "made no
  speeches; the party managed the campaign." (C)
- **Party machinery and partisan press dominated.** In 1860 the Republicans
  ran speakers, posters, leaflets, "thousands of newspaper editorials,"
  parades, rallies, surrogate stump speeches, and Election Day voter drives
  in taverns. The "Wide Awakes" — paramilitary young-Republican marching
  clubs — were a distinctive 1860 innovation. (C, per OAH / Roosevelt House)
- **Senate elections were still state-legislature elections.** The 1858
  Lincoln-Douglas race: Republican legislative candidates got ~53% of the
  statewide popular vote (~125,000 R vs ~121,000 D), but the malapportioned
  Illinois legislature chose Douglas 54–46. (C) **This is binding for AMPU
  Senate-context magnitudes in BOTH scenarios** — Senate elections in 1772
  and 1856 are NOT direct popular elections.
- **Oratory genuinely mattered, but mostly in Congress rather than on the
  campaign trail.** Webster-Hayne (Jan 19–27, 1830): Senate galleries packed,
  Webster's "Second Reply to Hayne" was widely read as a national speech and
  is still called "the most famous speech in Senate history" by senate.gov.
  (C) Lincoln-Douglas debates 1858: text printed in newspapers nationwide,
  collected into a book that sold ~30,000 copies in the first year. (C)
- **Sectional violence was within the political process.** Brooks caned
  Sumner on the Senate floor May 22, 1856 — same year as the scenario start.
  Sumner did not return full-time until December 1859. (C, per senate.gov)
  Convict-and-fine consequence: Brooks fined $300. Politically, Brooks was a
  Southern hero; in the North, the incident drove Republican mobilization.

## Per-trait grounding (15 traits)

### Charismatic

- **Historical examples:**
  - Henry Clay — "The Western Star," renowned for personal magnetism;
    nicknamed "The Great Compromiser." (C) Lost three presidential bids
    (1824, 1832, 1844) despite charisma — caveat: charisma is **necessary
    but not sufficient** in this period.
  - Patrick Henry — Great-Awakening-style spontaneous stump speaker, drew
    crowds across Virginia in 1770s–80s. (C)
  - William Jennings Bryan (1896 onward) — "introduced widespread stumping
    to presidential campaigning" with the Cross of Gold speech. (C)
    **Anachronism flag:** Bryan is post-1880, outside both AMPU windows.
- **Reference mechanic (from `source-abilities-expertise-traits.md`):**
  "magnetic appeal (looks + personality + enrapturing speaking style),
  recognized even by opponents; some voters are drawn to charisma over party
  or ideology."
- **Election-context applicability:** Strongest in **Governor**, **House**
  (mid-info, district), **Presidential general** (especially with public
  rallies in 1856 era), modest in **Senate** when popularly contested.
  **Weak in pre-17th-Amendment Senate** (state legislators are pros, less
  swayed by personal magnetism). Strong in **internal party / convention**
  contexts (1852 Pierce nomination, 1860 Lincoln nomination both turned on
  factional vote management, where personal appeal mattered).
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + medium across most contexts;
  **smaller** in pre-17 Senate (1772 + 1856 both) than in House/Governor/
  Presidential.
- **Anachronism flag:** none — works in both eras.
- **Sources:** [Britannica — Henry Clay](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Henry-Clay/The-Whig-Party-years-and-disappointment), [American Battlefield Trust — Patrick Henry](https://www.battlefields.org/learn/biographies/patrick-henry), [Britannica — Cross of Gold](https://www.britannica.com/event/Cross-of-Gold-speech)

### Integrity

- **Historical examples:**
  - Lincoln's "Honest Abe" nickname originated in New Salem (1830s) from
    business and judicial-honesty stories; the Republican Party
    **strategically deployed it in 1860**, with newspapers arguing "one of
    the strongest arguments in favor of the election of Lincoln to the
    Presidency is his HONESTY." (C) The branding WAS campaign-deployed, not
    merely retrospective — but Lincoln's actual political record involved
    plenty of standard 19th-century horse-trading. (R)
  - John Quincy Adams (post-presidency, as "Old Man Eloquent") cultivated a
    Congressional reputation for moral inflexibility on slavery / the gag
    rule — partly turned defeat (1828) into stature. (C)
- **Reference mechanic:** "adheres tightly to right and wrong even at
  personal cost; breaks with their party over fundamental moral lines;
  recognized even by opponents as honest and incorruptible."
- **Election-context applicability:** All contexts. Strongest when the
  **opponent has Scandalous, Controversial, or Corrupt** (the reference
  explicitly says Controversial is in "particular danger against an opponent
  known for Integrity").
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + small flat baseline, **bonus stacks
  when matched against a tainted opponent**.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [House Divided — Honest Abe](https://housedivided.dickinson.edu/sites/lincoln/honest-abe/), [Smithsonian — When Lincoln was more a politician than an Honest Abe](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/honest-abe-wasnt-always-quite-so-honest-180962194/)

### Debater

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Lincoln-Douglas 1858** — seven debates Aug–Oct 1858; texts
    nationally republished, ~30,000-copy book in first year; made Lincoln a
    national figure DESPITE losing the Senate vote. (C) **In-window for
    1856 scenario.**
  - **Webster–Hayne 1830** — Jan 19–27; Webster's Second Reply considered
    senate.gov "the most famous speech in Senate history." Webster
    consolidated national reputation; Hayne's lost reputation, paradoxically,
    led him to the South Carolina governorship in 1832. (C)
  - **Patrick Henry** — pre-revolutionary debate at Virginia conventions, the
    1775 "Give me liberty or give me death" speech being the canonical
    example. (C)
- **Reference mechanic:** "incisive debater; finds the best rhetorical
  methods to defend their ideas and attack an opponent's."
- **Election-context applicability:** Distinct from Orator. Debater is
  **opponent-conditional** (only matters when a candidate has a debate-style
  encounter). In 1772: most relevant in Continental Congress / convention
  voting, in 1856 era: most relevant in pre-17 Senate races contested at the
  legislative level AND in convention contests.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + medium **only in debate-bearing
  contexts** (head-to-head presidential, contested Senate races where
  debates occurred — Lincoln-Douglas is the model — and convention floor
  fights). Flat baseline otherwise should be small or zero.
- **Anachronism flag:** none — but most powerful in 1856-era Senate /
  presidential, since the formalized debate tour was a 1850s+ invention.
- **Sources:** [senate.gov — The Most Famous Senate Speech](https://www.senate.gov/artandhistory/history/minute/The_Most_Famous_Senate_Speech.htm), [NPS — Lincoln-Douglas Debates](https://www.nps.gov/liho/learn/historyculture/debates.htm), [Britannica — Lincoln-Douglas debates](https://www.britannica.com/event/Lincoln-Douglas-debates)

### Propagandist

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Hamilton** — Federalist Papers (51 of 85 essays under "Publius") +
    later the Reports on Public Credit / Manufactures. (C) National-press
    operator par excellence in the 1787–1791 window.
  - **Thomas Paine** — *Common Sense* (1776) — 500,000 copies / ~20% of
    colonists owned one. (C) Pamphlet warfare was the medium of the era.
  - **Republican campaign newspapers in 1860** — "Lincoln and Liberty!!"
    published Jun 19 – Oct 2, 1860. The Wide Awakes' printed material,
    "thousands of editorials." (C)
- **Reference mechanic:** "mastered broadcasting their/their faction's
  opinions via the mass communication of their era; may use misleading or
  cherry-picked info; the worst create bold-faced lies established as
  truths."
- **Election-context applicability:** Cuts across contexts but expresses
  through PARTY-LEVEL impact, not personal vote share. **Highest in
  multi-state / national contests** (presidential general, multi-state
  ratification-style votes). Lower per-district House impact unless they
  control the local press.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + medium in presidential general / +
  small in House and Senate races. Context-conditional rather than flat —
  more effect when print media is the campaign medium (i.e., always, in both
  AMPU windows).
- **Anachronism flag:** **Term "Propagandist" is anachronistic** (the term
  enters English in modern form c. 1842 and political-pejorative sense
  c. 1908–1914 — verify if PM cares about literal naming). The CONCEPT
  (partisan-press operators, pamphleteers) is fully native to both 1772 and
  1856 windows.
- **Sources:** [Smithsonian — Common Sense](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/smart-news/americas-first-viral-post-was-published-on-this-day-in-1776-when-thomas-paines-common-sense-sparked-a-revolution-180985802/), [Battlefields — Federalist Papers](https://www.battlefields.org/learn/articles/federalist-papers), [Roosevelt House — Origins of Modern Campaigning](https://www.roosevelthouse.hunter.cuny.edu/seehowtheyran/portfolios/origins-of-modern-campaigning/)

### Harmonious

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Henry Clay — "The Great Compromiser."** Authored / brokered the
    Missouri Compromise (1820), the Compromise Tariff (1833), and the
    Compromise of 1850. (C) Clay's career is the canonical antebellum
    example.
  - **James Monroe** — Presided over the "Era of Good Feelings" 1815–1825;
    1820 reelection was unanimous except one dissenting NH elector. (C) The
    Era of Good Feelings was as much a post-collapse-of-Federalist phenomenon
    as a Monroe achievement, but he was its face.
  - **Franklin Pierce 1852** — dark-horse nominee whose appeal was precisely
    that "having few enemies, nor indeed any reputation, Pierce could be
    molded by his supporters into whatever the delegates were looking for."
    (C) Won on the 49th ballot. Pierce is a Harmonious-style WIN in a
    convention context.
- **Reference mechanic:** "seeks the middle ground; more liable to cave to
  avoid disruption; less partisan, but not so bipartisan as to anger the
  base; aims for no drama."
- **Election-context applicability:** Strongest in **internal-party /
  convention / Senate-legislature** contexts where consensus-building wins
  (1852 nomination, 1820 unanimity). Weaker — possibly mildly negative — in
  high-polarization primary contests where the base wants a fighter.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + small in popular elections, + medium
  in convention / multi-ballot / Senate-legislature contexts.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [Henry Clay Estate](https://henryclay.org/learn/about-henry-clay/), [World History — Henry Clay](https://www.worldhistory.org/Henry_Clay/), [Britannica — Era of Good Feelings](https://www.britannica.com/event/Era-of-Good-Feelings), [1852 Democratic National Convention (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1852_Democratic_National_Convention)

### Unlikable

- **Historical examples:**
  - **John Adams** — described by contemporaries as vain, stubborn,
    cantankerous, "wore his thoughts on his sleeve," with an "ungovernable
    temper." (C) Lost 1800 re-election — the first incumbent to do so —
    partly because Hamilton's leaked criticism of Adams' character
    damaged him within his own party. (C) **Personality unlikability had
    concrete electoral consequence here.**
  - **John Quincy Adams** — prickly Puritan-aristocrat reputation; lost
    1828 in a landslide to Jackson partly on personality grounds (the
    Jacksonian press painted him "an unscrupulous aristocrat"). (C)
- **Reference mechanic:** "strongly disliked even by a noticeable fraction
  of their own party; allied politicians dislike them personally; can
  increase opposition turnout more than their own."
- **Election-context applicability:** All contexts. Strongest in
  **presidential general** (high-info / personality-aware electorate) and
  in **internal party / leadership** contests where allies refuse to
  cooperate.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – small to – medium flat, with a
  **larger swing when matched against an opponent with Charismatic or
  Likable**.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [Battlefields — Election of 1800](https://www.battlefields.org/learn/articles/election-1800-adams-vs-jefferson), [Britannica — Election of 1828](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1828)

### Puritan

- **Historical examples:**
  - **William Lloyd Garrison** — uncompromising abolitionist; "NO
    COMPROMISE WITH SLAVERY! NO UNION WITH SLAVEHOLDERS!"; favored Northern
    secession from the Union as a moral matter; rejected political
    engagement as morally compromised. (C) Drew followers but was
    politically ineffective by design.
  - **Charles Sumner** — Massachusetts senator caned on the Senate floor
    May 22, 1856 for an "invective-laden" anti-slavery speech. (C) His
    moral inflexibility made him a martyr in the North and a hated figure
    in the South — i.e., the trait's both-edges nature.
  - **John Quincy Adams** in his post-presidency anti-slavery crusade — see
    Integrity above.
- **Reference mechanic:** "unbending devotion to ideology; exists to promote
  their ideals; sees little merit in compromise even when compromise prevents
  the opposite outcome; cult following but ineffective party/national
  leader."
- **Election-context applicability:** Mixed. **Negative in convention /
  consensus / Senate-legislature contexts** (Sumner's cohort had hostile
  Democratic legislatures to fight in some periods); potentially **positive
  in ideologically-aligned faction primaries** where moral purity is a
  selling point.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – small flat baseline in popular
  general elections, – medium in convention / coalition-building contexts;
  **could be positive in same-ideology faction primary** contexts.
- **Anachronism flag:** Concept is era-spanning (literal Puritans c. 1620;
  the abolitionist Puritan archetype is mid-1800s, in-window for 1856; not
  out of place in 1772 thanks to New England Congregationalist political
  inheritance).
- **Sources:** [Libertarianism.org — Garrison/Douglass on Disunionism](https://www.libertarianism.org/columns/william-lloyd-garrison-frederick-douglass-disunionism), [Wikipedia — Caning of Charles Sumner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caning_of_Charles_Sumner)

### Numberfudger

- **Historical examples:**
  - The reference frames this as the inverse of Bookkeeper. The clearest
    historical inversion is the **Hamilton-Reynolds blackmail** case: when
    Hamilton's Treasury record was attacked in 1797, opponents (Monroe,
    Muhlenberg, Venable) initially mistook the Reynolds blackmail payments
    for evidence of Treasury malfeasance. (C) Hamilton was actually
    competent at numbers; the accusation that he was Numberfudger was
    politically damaging UNTIL the affair pamphlet refuted it.
  - **(I)** I couldn't find a clean canonical Numberfudger from the 1772 or
    1856 window. The reference's example is more clearly post-1860 (Grant
    Treasury appointments, gold-corner scandals etc.).
- **Reference mechanic:** "laughably incompetent with colossal economic
  duties even if otherwise a great administrator; should not be Sec of
  Treasury."
- **Election-context applicability:** Cuts narrowly. Not a general-election
  swing trait — most voters don't audit Treasury records. **Bites primarily
  in (a) internal party / cabinet-confirmation contexts and (b) post-
  scandal re-election cycles** where economic mismanagement is a campaign
  issue (e.g., Panic of 1857 — see `eraEvents1856.ts`).
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – very small in normal elections, –
  larger only in panic/recession-context elections.
- **Anachronism flag:** none — concept fits both eras.
- **Sources:** [PBS — Duel: Maria Reynolds](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/features/duel-maria-reynolds/)

### Scandalous

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Hamilton-Reynolds (affair 1791–92, pamphlet 1797).** America's first
    political sex scandal. "Permanently ended any hope he might have had of
    becoming president of the United States," per History.com. (C) Note
    this was an EXECUTIVE-aspiration killer, NOT a House/Senate-disqualifier:
    Hamilton hadn't been seeking elective office.
  - **The Eaton Affair / Petticoat Affair (1829–31).** Wife of Sec War John
    Eaton was rumored sexually promiscuous; cabinet wives shunned the
    Eatons; Jackson defended them; eventually used the affair as cover to
    reorganize the cabinet and break with Calhoun. (C) Martin Van Buren
    emerged stronger; John Calhoun's national prospects were damaged.
  - **Jackson's marriage attacks 1828** — opposition press painted Jackson
    as adulterer/bigamist because Rachel's first divorce hadn't been
    finalized when they married. (C) Jackson still won 56% of popular vote,
    68% of electoral. Lesson: **scandal does NOT auto-defeat; it depends on
    counter-narrative and party machinery.** (R)
- **Reference mechanic:** "Scandalous" isn't in the source's trait
  definitions list — closest is **Corrupt** (paired in TRAIT_CONFLICTS) and
  the Scandals event-effects bucket. In AMPU's existing engine the trait
  appears in the `NEGATIVE_TRAITS` array. For PR4a treat as a public-image
  hit narrower than Controversial (sex/finance) and narrower than Corrupt
  (which implies in-office malfeasance).
- **Election-context applicability:** All contexts but with significant
  variance. **Strongest in House / Governor** (district-level, sticky local
  reputation). **In presidential general**, may be cancelled by Teflon /
  party machinery; Jackson 1828 is the proof case.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – medium flat; **modulated up if
  opponent has Integrity, down if same politician has Teflon**.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [History.com — Scandal That Ruined Hamilton](https://www.history.com/articles/alexander-hamilton-maria-reynolds-pamphlet-affair), [HistoryNet — Petticoat Affair](https://historynet.com/andrew-jackson-the-petticoat-affair-scandal-in-jackons-white-house/), [Boundary Stones — Election of 1828](https://boundarystones.weta.org/2017/01/13/election-1828-its-always-been-ugly)

### Controversial

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Andrew Jackson (entire career).** Killed Charles Dickinson in 1806
    duel; "gambler, duelist, adulterer and military tyrant" was 1828
    opposition framing; ignored Worcester v Georgia; "Bank War"; etc. Yet
    won decisively in 1828 and 1832. **Controversy is electorally
    mobilizing, not just dampening.** (C)
  - **Stephen A. Douglas** post-Kansas-Nebraska (1854): his sponsorship of
    popular sovereignty made him uniquely controversial — drove
    Republican-party formation against him AND won him 1860 northern-
    Democratic nomination. (C)
  - **Preston Brooks** post-caning (May 1856): convicted of assault, fined
    $300, **but re-elected** by his South Carolina district — controversy
    cuts both ways. (C)
- **Reference mechanic:** "reputation for saying/doing morally wrong
  things; harder to get elected; in particular danger against an opponent
  known for Integrity."
- **Election-context applicability:** All contexts. The reference is
  explicit that Integrity is the natural foil — this is the PM's clearest
  Charismatic-Unlikable–style opposing-trait interaction.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – medium flat, but with **substantial
  regional/factional variance**: Controversial Southerners running in the
  1856-era South register positively in same-faction primaries.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [Boundary Stones — Election of 1828](https://boundarystones.weta.org/2017/01/13/election-1828-its-always-been-ugly), [Wikipedia — Caning of Sumner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caning_of_Charles_Sumner)

### Obscure

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Franklin Pierce (1852)** — did not receive a vote on any of first 34
    ballots at the Democratic convention; won on the 49th. Many delegates
    "had never even heard of Pierce" — and this was precisely what got him
    nominated as a compromise. (C) **In-window for 1856.** Pierce is the
    canonical Obscure-cuts-through-nominations example.
  - **James K. Polk (1844)** — "the first 'dark horse'"; got the
    Democratic nomination on the 9th ballot after Van Buren's Texas
    annexation stance disqualified him. (C) Like Pierce, Polk's obscurity
    was an asset in a deadlocked convention.
- **Reference mechanic:** "most reliable voters have no clue who they are;
  name recognition confined to (or unknown even within) their state."
- **Election-context applicability:** **Negative in popular elections**
  (House, Senate popular vote, governor, presidential general). **Can be
  POSITIVE in deadlocked-convention / multi-ballot internal party
  contests** — the historical pattern is that obscurity = "few enemies" =
  acceptable to all factions. **In Senate-legislature contests
  (1772+1856)**, obscurity to the broad public is irrelevant; what matters
  is being known to the ~100 legislators. (R)
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – medium in popular contexts, **+
  small in deadlocked multi-faction convention contexts**, neutral in
  pre-17 Senate.
- **Anachronism flag:** none.
- **Sources:** [1852 Democratic National Convention (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1852_Democratic_National_Convention), [LOC — Election of 1844](https://guides.loc.gov/presidential-election-1844)

### Domestic Apathy

- **Historical examples:**
  - **John Quincy Adams 1825–1829.** Brilliant Sec of State (Monroe
    Doctrine, Adams-Onis Treaty). As President, his foreign-affairs strength
    didn't translate; he was "prevented from resolving the ongoing issue of
    trade with the British West Indies" and "rivals in Congress were
    determined to deny him any mark of success." Lost 1828 in a landslide.
    (C)
  - **John Jay** — diplomat (Jay Treaty 1794); when he ran for NY governor
    he was strikingly less effective in retail domestic politics than in
    diplomatic chambers. (R)
- **Reference mechanic:** "chronic neglect of/limited empathy for domestic
  issues; regardless of admin skill, better in foreign affairs, military,
  or trade posts."
- **Election-context applicability:** **Negative in GOVERNOR and HOUSE**
  contexts (most domestic / district-level). **Neutral in Senate and
  Presidential general** in the 1772 window (high foreign-policy salience
  during Federalist era). **Increasingly negative in 1856 window** —
  domestic issues (slavery, tariff) dominate so the trait should cost more.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – small in 1772 window, – medium in
  1856 window; – larger for Governor / House than Senate / President.
- **Anachronism flag:** **Term "Domestic Apathy" is modern/awkward
  phrasing**, but the underlying disposition (foreign-policy aristocrats
  bored by tariff debates) is real in both eras. PM may want to consider
  a more period-appropriate label for display purposes (e.g.
  "Foreign-Looking", "Diplomatist").
- **Sources:** [Britannica — JQ Adams](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Quincy-Adams), [Miller Center — JQ Adams: Foreign Affairs](https://millercenter.org/president/jqadams/foreign-affairs)

### Carpetbagger

- **Historical examples:**
  - **The term itself is Reconstruction-specific.** First known use: 1867
    (per Merriam-Webster). Refers to Northerners who moved south after
    the Civil War carrying a carpet-bag — i.e., the implication that all
    their property qualifications fit in a carpet-bag and they sought
    office via post-war Republican Party machinery. (C)
  - **For 1772, the concept of "non-resident representation" did exist** —
    colonial elections had disputes over candidates not living in the
    constituency, and several colonies/states codified residency
    requirements in response. (C) But there was no general slur tagged to
    it.
  - **For 1856 — antebellum**, "carpetbagger" is not yet a term in use.
    Migration into Western territories and "Border Ruffians" coming into
    Kansas were the relevant 1854–58 phenomenon, but the political-stigma
    label is post-1865.
- **Reference mechanic:** "recently moved to a new state hoping to gain
  office; their ambition is transparent and their manner alien to locals,
  so they struggle to establish themselves in the new state."
- **Election-context applicability:** Narrow. By design only cuts at the
  **state level** — Governor, House (especially in fresh districts),
  pre-17 Senate (state legislators know who the carpetbaggers are).
  Irrelevant at presidential general.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** – medium in Governor / House
  contexts when politician is running outside birth/long-tenure state;
  null elsewhere. This already integrates with `RELOCATION_ODDS`
  and the `CARPETBAGGER_LADDER` in `src/types.ts`.
- **Anachronism flag:** **Strong anachronism for 1772; mild anachronism
  for 1856.** Term itself is Reconstruction-only. Existing AMPU engine
  uses the trait as a label tied to relocation mechanics — this is
  defensible as a UX label but the PM should know the term is
  era-incorrect.
- **Sources:** [Merriam-Webster — Carpetbagger](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/carpetbagger), [Etymonline — Carpetbagger](https://www.etymonline.com/word/carpetbagger), [Wikipedia — Carpetbagger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carpetbagger)

### Outsider

- **Historical examples:**
  - **Andrew Jackson 1828.** Frontier outsider attacking the "corrupt
    bargain" of 1824 (Adams + Clay). Won 56% of popular vote, 68% of
    electoral. (C) "Jacksonian Democracy" is the canonical pre-Civil War
    outsider populist arc; campaign 1828 reframed Adams as "an
    unscrupulous aristocrat."
  - **William Henry Harrison 1840.** The Log Cabin and Hard Cider
    campaign explicitly remade aristocratic-born Harrison into an outsider
    frontier candidate. (C) Won 1840.
  - **John Charles Frémont 1856.** Westerner; first Republican
    presidential nominee. Lost to Buchanan but won 11 of 16 northern
    states — outsider candidacy as nascent-party tool. (R)
- **Reference mechanic:** Not in the source ability/trait reference;
  AMPU-specific label. Conceptually: against-the-machine populist appeal.
- **Election-context applicability:** Strongest in **Presidential general**
  in periods of electorate anti-establishment mood (1828, 1840). Helps in
  **state-level open seats** where the political class is unpopular. Hurts
  in **pre-17 Senate (state legislatures)** and **convention** contests
  because legislators / party regulars ARE the establishment.
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + small in popular elections, **–
  small in pre-17 Senate** and convention/internal contests, modulated by
  the era's anti-establishment mood (Jacksonian post-1824, ~free-soil
  post-1854).
- **Anachronism flag:** Modern term; the **concept** is in-window for 1856
  (Jacksonian arc, Free Soil 1848, Republican formation 1854–56) and
  partially for late 1772 window (1800 Jeffersonian "outsider" rhetoric
  against the Adams Federalists, although both candidates were Founders).
- **Sources:** [Miller Center — Jackson Campaigns](https://millercenter.org/president/jackson/campaigns-and-elections), [Britannica — Election of 1840](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1840)

### Magician

- **Historical examples (canon, from AMPU's own designer notes):**
  - **Martin Van Buren** — "Little Magician" / "Sly Fox"; built the
    Albany Regency; key architect of Jackson's Democratic Party; emerged
    from the Eaton Affair stronger; engineered cabinet reorganization
    1831; brokered his way to the presidency 1836. (C) **The eponymous
    case** per the AMPU canon list.
  - **Aaron Burr.** Tied Jefferson 73-73 in the 1800 EC, manipulated
    New York delegations to extract that result, held out in the House
    contingent vote to extract concessions — failed only because of
    Hamilton's intervention. (C) Burr is the Founding-era Magician
    archetype.
  - **Stephen A. Douglas** — Kansas-Nebraska Act 1854 was a legislative
    masterstroke (and political disaster). Won the 1858 Senate seat by
    legislative-vote math despite popular-vote loss. (C) In-window for
    1856.
- **Reference mechanic:** "generational legislative strategist, capable of
  legislative tricks the top ranks cannot do; shows promise before
  mastering legislating. Named after Martin Van Buren ('The Little
  Magician'). Rare trait."
- **Election-context applicability:** Highest in **pre-17 Senate** (state
  legislature math — Douglas 1858 is the proof case), **internal party /
  convention / faction-leader / Speaker / floor votes**. Lower in
  popular-election general contexts (a Magician's tricks don't move
  general-electorate vote share much).
- **Suggested magnitude direction:** + medium in legislative / convention /
  Speaker / Senate-legislature contests, + small in popular elections.
- **Anachronism flag:** none — the canon spans 1789 (Gerry/Burr) through
  modern (LBJ, Gingrich, McConnell).
- **Sources:** [Boston Globe — The Little Magician](https://www.bostonglobe.com/2022/02/20/opinion/little-magician-who-transformed-american-politics/), [American Heritage — Rise of the Little Magician](https://www.americanheritage.com/rise-little-magician), [Smithsonian — Jefferson, Burr, and 1800](https://www.smithsonianmag.com/history/thomas-jefferson-aaron-burr-and-the-election-of-1800-131082359/), [Britannica — Stephen A. Douglas](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Stephen-A-Douglas)

## Cross-trait observations

- **Pre-17th-Amendment Senate is the load-bearing election-context gate for
  BOTH AMPU scenarios.** Senate elections in 1772 and 1856 are decided by
  state legislatures, not popular vote. Charisma, Outsider, Scandalous,
  Carpetbagger should all be **substantially dampened** in Senate context
  for both scenarios. Magician, Debater, Harmonious, and arguably Obscure
  (as a compromise candidate) should be **amplified** there.
- **Presidential primaries as the game models them (the "primaries phase")
  did not exist in either AMPU scenario window.** Primary elections began
  in 1900s Progressive era; binding presidential primaries are a 1972
  post-McGovern-Fraser-Commission phenomenon. (C) For pre-1900 traits, the
  PM should treat the AMPU "primary" phase as analogous to the **party
  convention process** (1852 Pierce, 1844 Polk, 1860 Lincoln), where
  Magician, Harmonious, and Obscure historically had outsized advantage,
  and Charismatic / Debater mattered too but less than in popular elections.
- **The mismatch between "Scandalous-as-career-ender" (Hamilton 1797) and
  "Scandalous-as-shrugged-off" (Jackson 1828) is the most important
  modulating fact for the negative-character cluster.** Counter-trait
  (Teflon) and party machinery moderate the magnitude. Per `pv.ts:73-76`,
  the existing PV currently gives every negative trait a flat -5 — for
  PR4a the PM may want context-conditional negative magnitudes rather than
  flat values.
- **The 1840 Log Cabin campaign is the inflection point** for the modern
  campaign style. The 1772 scenario should weight party-press traits
  (Propagandist) more heavily; the 1856 scenario should also support stump-
  oratory mechanics (Charismatic, Orator, Debater) more weight.
- **Three "negative-character" traits in this PR4a scope (Unlikable,
  Controversial, Scandalous) overlap heavily.** Historical examples for one
  are often examples for all three (Jackson 1828 hits Controversial AND
  Scandalous; Adams 1800 hits Unlikable; Hamilton 1797 hits Scandalous).
  The PM should make sure their magnitudes don't double-dip on the same
  political phenomenon. (I)
- **Female pronouns and identity terms.** Both AMPU windows predate women's
  suffrage (1920) and the 1965 Voting Rights Act. The PM should be aware
  that the entire electorate in both scenarios is white-male-propertied
  initially. This isn't a trait-mechanic concern per se but it shapes
  WHICH voter blocs charisma/integrity/etc. flow to.

## Open questions for the PM

1. **Does AMPU's "primaries phase" pre-1900 represent (a) the convention
   process (which existed) or (b) an anachronistic transplant of modern
   primaries?** This affects whether Magician/Harmonious/Obscure get
   their historically-justified primary bonus in pre-1900 phases.
2. **Should the Carpetbagger trait label be era-aware (renamed in the
   1772 scenario)?** The term is Reconstruction-era; the mechanic is fine
   for both eras but the label collision is jarring for 1772 players.
3. **For Scandalous: should the Teflon counter-trait downward modulation
   be in PR4a scope or deferred to a later trait pass?** The historical
   record (Hamilton vs Jackson) is the strongest case for context-aware
   negative-trait magnitudes.
4. **For Debater: should magnitude only fire in years with a documented
   "debate event" (Webster-Hayne, Lincoln-Douglas, presidential
   debates)?** The trait's historical impact was always episode-bound,
   not flat.
5. **Outsider — is the modulating "anti-establishment mood" meter
   computable from existing GameState** (e.g., interest groups, party
   preference, era flags), or does it need a new signal? The 1828 and
   1854–56 contexts are the historical anchors.
6. **Domestic Apathy label — does the PM want to keep the modern
   phrasing for UX consistency**, or rename per era? (See anachronism
   table.)
7. **Numberfudger** — historical examples are thin for both windows;
   the trait may be a forward-looking design slot rather than a
   historically-anchored one for 1772/1856. The PM may want to keep
   magnitudes small and recheck for the Gilded Age / New Deal era.
