## 2. Binding facts the 1820 start needs (treat as ground truth)

These supplement parent §2.5's binding facts (which already cover the Revolution of
1800, Louisiana Purchase, the embargo/War of 1812, and the Missouri Compromise).

- **The 1820 election — Monroe ran essentially unopposed and won 231–1.**
  **[consensus]** Monroe (Democratic-Republican) took **231 of 232 electoral votes**;
  the sole dissent was faithless elector **William Plumer of New Hampshire**, who
  voted for **John Quincy Adams**. The popular legend that Plumer dissented to
  preserve Washington's unique status as the only unanimous president is **false** —
  Plumer simply thought Monroe mediocre and Adams better. This is the *third and last*
  time a presidential candidate ran effectively unopposed. ([Britannica — 1820 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1820), [Annenberg Classroom — An Elector Changes His Vote](https://www.annenbergclassroom.org/timeline_event/an-elector-changes-his-vote/)) *(The 1820 start opens in the aftermath of exactly this election — "almost unanimously reelected… running virtually unopposed," chunk-001 post 1.)*
- **The 1816 election that broke the Federalists — Monroe beat Rufus King 183–34.**
  **[consensus]** Monroe/Tompkins (Dem-Rep) defeated the de facto Federalist King,
  whose party was in shambles after the **Dec 1814–Jan 1815 Hartford Convention** and
  the Treaty of Ghent. ([Britannica — 1816 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1816), [National Archives — 1816 Electoral College](https://www.archives.gov/electoral-college/1816)) *(The GA's opening post states King "lost 183-34 to Monroe, receiving only about 31% of the popular vote" and was "the first Presidential candidate to speak out against slavery, which cost him almost all his support in the South" — the electoral figures are correct; the antislavery-stance claim is a defensible characterization of King, who did oppose slavery's extension, though "first" is a stretch.)*
- **The Panic of 1819 was the first major U.S. financial depression.** **[consensus]**
  It struck two years into Monroe's presidency, during the "Era of Good Feelings."
  Causes: the post-Napoleonic-Wars collapse of global demand and commodity/agricultural
  prices, over-speculation in Western land, and — critically — the newly chartered
  **Second Bank of the United States** sharply **contracting credit** (calling in
  loans, demanding specie from state banks). Result: bank failures, foreclosures,
  urban unemployment, and a surge of popular resentment toward banks and speculators
  that **fractured the Democratic-Republicans over economic policy** and fed the
  democratic/anti-bank politics of the coming decade. Monroe could do little to
  blunt it. ([Wikipedia — Panic of 1819](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1819), [Economic Historian — Panic of 1819 as America's First Great Depression](https://www.economic-historian.com/p/panic-of-1819)) *(The opening post leans heavily on this — "deep and profound impact… first major financial crisis… weakened the dominant Democratic-Republican Party by exposing internal divisions over economic policy," chunk-001 post 1.)*
- **The Monroe Doctrine — December 2, 1823.** **[consensus]** First stated in
  Monroe's **seventh annual message to Congress (Dec 2, 1823)**. Three core
  principles: (1) **separate spheres** for the Americas and Europe; (2)
  **non-colonization** — "the American continents… are henceforth not to be
  considered as subjects for future colonization by any European Power"; (3)
  **non-intervention** — European interference in the independent New World republics
  would be viewed as hostile to the U.S., and in return the U.S. would stay out of
  European wars. **Secretary of State John Quincy Adams** was the principal architect:
  he argued for a *unilateral* U.S. statement rather than a joint declaration with
  Britain, and his view prevailed. Crucially, **the U.S. lacked the military power to
  enforce it in 1823** — its teeth came from tacit alignment with the British Royal
  Navy, which had its own reasons to keep other European powers out of Latin America.
  ([National Archives — Monroe Doctrine (1823)](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/monroe-doctrine), [State Dept Office of the Historian — Monroe Doctrine, 1823](https://history.state.gov/milestones/1801-1829/monroe), [Britannica — Monroe Doctrine](https://www.britannica.com/event/Monroe-Doctrine)) *(The opening post foreshadows it without naming it — Monroe "favors the independence of several former European colonies… it wouldn't be a surprise if he warned European powers against further colonization or interference in the affairs of the Western Hemisphere," chunk-001 post 1. In-game it had not yet fired as of the early-1820s point the playtest reached.)*
- **The "National Republican" label is anachronistic in 1820 — the party did not
  meaningfully exist until ~1825, and the name itself dates from ~1830.**
  **[consensus]** There was **no organized "National Republican Party" in 1820**. The
  faction grew out of the Democratic-Republicans who backed **John Quincy Adams** in
  the 1824 election (initially "Adams–Clay Republicans" / "Adams's Men," 1825–29);
  the **name "National Republican" came into use around 1830**, after the 1828 loss
  to Jackson made them the formal anti-Jackson opposition (Clay replacing Adams as
  leader). They became the **Whigs ~1833–34**. ([Wikipedia — National Republican Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Republican_Party), [Britannica — National Republican Party](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Republican-Party)) *(See divergence note: the GA's "brand new National Republican Party (Red)" in 1820 telescopes a party that was 5–10 years away into the start state — a deliberate game convenience, but historically the RED party in 1820 was the **dying Federalists**, not National Republicans.)*
- **The "Era of Democracy" name = the expansion of white-male suffrage.**
  **[consensus]** The period's defining democratic shift was the **dropping of
  property and taxpaying qualifications for voting**, extending the franchise to
  nearly all **adult white men**. Most older states removed property requirements
  between ~1810 and the mid-1820s; by **1840** almost all white men could vote in all
  but three states (RI, VA, and — per some accounts — LA/NC). This is the suffrage
  story that gives the era its "**Era of Democracy**" name and fuels the rise of mass
  party politics and Jackson's 1828 coalition. **Neutral framing:** the franchise
  expanded for **white men** while **race replaced property** as the criterion —
  several states simultaneously **stripped voting rights from free Black men**, and
  women remained disfranchised everywhere. Describe it as the franchise mechanic it
  was, without modern editorializing. ([Gilder Lehrman — Voting Rights and Restrictions in Pre-Emancipation America](https://www.gilderlehrman.org/history-resources/essays/voting-rights-and-restrictions-pre-emancipation-america), [National Humanities Center — The Expansion of Democracy during the Jacksonian Era](https://americainclass.org/the-expansion-of-democracy-during-the-jacksonian-era/)) *(The opening post names this directly as a wedge issue — the Jeffersonian wing believed in "extending the right to vote to every white male, no matter if they were land holders or not," chunk-001 post 1.)*
- **The spoils system was Jacksonian (post-1828), NOT Monroe-era.** **[consensus]**
  Under the long run of Virginia Dem-Rep presidents, **permanent tenure** in federal
  office was the de facto norm; J.Q. Adams refused to remove even opponents. The
  **"spoils system" / "rotation in office"** is a **Jackson administration**
  development: on taking office in **1829**, Jackson dismissed ~**900 federal
  officeholders (nearly 10%)** for partisan allies, justified as "rotation in office."
  The phrase **"to the victor belong the spoils"** comes from Sen. **William L. Marcy
  in 1832** (defending Jackson's removals), not from Jackson himself. ([Britannica — Spoils system](https://www.britannica.com/topic/spoils-system), [Miller Center — Jackson: Domestic Affairs](https://millercenter.org/president/jackson/domestic-affairs)) *(Relevant because a 1820 start that runs forward will reach this; but at the 1820 start itself, the patronage norm is still Virginia-dynasty permanent tenure, not spoils.)*

## 3. Key figures specific to the 1820 start

Parent §2.5 already covers Jefferson, Madison, Monroe, Burr, Gallatin, J.Q. Adams,
Hamilton, John Marshall, Andrew Jackson, and Henry Clay. The 1820 start's **live
draft** seats several of these as first-round picks; additions and notes below.

- **James Monroe** (Dem-Rep) — president 1817–25. The 1820 start opens in his second
  term; the figures the start hangs on him are the **Panic of 1819**, the **Missouri
  Compromise (1820)**, and the **Monroe Doctrine (1823)**. Drafted as a first-round
  pick (B4, "President James Monroe, VA," chunk-001 post 2). **[consensus]**
- **John Marshall** (Federalist Chief Justice, 1801–35) — the **first overall pick**
  of the 1820 live draft (R3, chunk-001 post 2), and later named a faction leader
  with the self-chosen label "**Marshall Federalists (National Republicans?)**"
  (chunk-003 post 224). His era-relevant rulings predate the start: *Marbury* (1803,
  judicial review) and *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819, upheld the Second Bank and
  "implied powers") — the latter directly relevant to the Panic-of-1819/Bank politics
  the start foregrounds. **[consensus]**
- **DeWitt Clinton** (Dem-Rep, NY) — drafted first by the LW/Prog/Lib BLUE faction
  (B1, "Gov DeWitt Clinton, NY," chunk-001 post 2; later faction-named "Liberal
  Democrats," chunk-003 post 225). Real-history: NY governor, champion of the **Erie
  Canal** (opened 1825) — i.e. the archetype of the **internal-improvements/
  "American System"-adjacent** wing the opening post describes as the party's
  "Liberal" camp. A good period-accurate avatar for that wing. **[consensus]**
- **Rufus King** (Federalist, NY) — drafted R1 (chunk-001 post 2). The **1816
  Federalist presidential nominee** and a genuine **antislavery** voice in the
  Missouri debates — the correct figure to anchor the dying RED/Federalist pole and
  the Northern-antislavery thread the Missouri Compromise raises. **[consensus]**
- **Nathaniel Macon** (Dem-Rep, NC) — drafted B5 (Trad/RW Pop, chunk-001 post 2).
  The archetypal **"Old Republican" / strict-construction** Southern agrarian — the
  right figure for the Jeffersonian small-government wing. **[consensus]**
- **Henry Clay** (KY) — drafted B2 (chunk-001 post 2); one player names a faction the
  "**Clay Democrats**" (chunk-003 post 221). Real-history: Speaker, "American System"
  (tariff/national bank/internal improvements), Missouri-Compromise broker, and the
  1824 "Corrupt Bargain" maker who becomes a **National Republican** then **Whig**.
  *Note the cross-party draft placement:* the game lets either party draft him; his
  real trajectory is the **nationalist (eventually anti-Jackson) wing**, not the
  Jacksonian Democrats. **[consensus]**
- **Daniel Webster** (MA) — drafted R5 (chunk-001 post 2). In 1820 still a younger
  Federalist-leaning nationalist; his Whig leadership and "Liberty and Union" oratory
  come later. Parent §2.6 flags the standing game divergence of labeling him "leader
  of the **Federalists**" as late as 1840 — at a **1820** start that label is at
  least era-plausible (the Federalists genuinely still existed regionally in 1820),
  unlike in 1840. **[consensus]**
- **Martin Van Buren** (Dem-Rep, NY) — *the playtest kills him off early*: an
  in-thread newspaper event reports "Senator Martin Van Buren Dies Suddenly at 38"
  in **July 1821** (chunk-004 post 272), removing the real architect of the
  Democratic Party before he can build it. Real-history: the **Albany Regency**
  organizer who builds the mass Democratic Party and becomes president 1837–41. His
  early death is a pure alt-history divergence (see notes). **[consensus on real role]**
- **James K. Polk** (drafted B5 rookie, chunk-001 post 25), **George M. Dallas**,
  **Thaddeus Stevens**, **John Tyler**, **James Buchanan** (a player names a faction
  "Arch-Federalists" around him, chunk-003 post 219), **Stephen F. Austin**, **Davy
  Crockett**, **Matthew C. Perry** — all appear as 1820-class rookies or
  career-track picks. These are **real figures of the right generation** for an 1820
  draft (born ~1790s), correctly seated as juniors a decade-plus before their
  national prominence. **[consensus]**

## 4. Era-appropriate factions / ideologies / interests (1820 lens)

The story is **one dominant party (Dem-Reps) beginning to fragment** under economic
(Panic of 1819) and sectional (Missouri) stress — *before* the clean Democrat/Whig
split of the next decade. The playtest's **B1–B5 / R1–R5** structure maps onto this:

- **BLUE = Democratic-Republicans** (dominant). The opening post splits them into:
  - a **"Liberal" wing** — internal improvements, government regulation, a
    **protective tariff**, and acceptance of the **Second Bank** (they "adopted many
    of the old Federalist positions"). This is the nationalist/American-System wing
    that historically becomes **National Republicans → Whigs**. In-game: the LW
    Pop/Prog/Lib factions (B1–B2; "Liberal Democrats," "Clay Democrats").
  - an **"RW Pop / Trad" Jeffersonian wing** — smaller government, lower taxes,
    strict construction, and **white-male suffrage expansion**. This is the
    "Old Republican"/states'-rights wing that historically becomes the core of the
    **Jacksonian Democrats**. In-game: B4–B5 (Cons/Trad/RW Pop).
- **RED = the dying Federalists** (a New England/Northeast rump) — and, per the GA's
  framing, the nascent "National Republican" pole. In-game R1–R5 span LW Pop→RW Pop,
  with players reaching for period labels ("Arch-Federalists," "National Republicans,"
  "Marshall Federalists"). *Historically the RED party in 1820 is genuinely the
  Federalists; the National Republican name is ~5–10 years premature (see §2 binding
  facts).*
- **Live interests** the era actually turned on: **Western farmers/settlers** (cheap
  land, expansion — hit hard by the Panic), **Southern planters** (slavery, low
  tariff), **Northern merchants/manufacturers** (protective tariff after 1816), the
  **Second Bank** as a political lightning rod, and the **new sectional fault line
  over slavery in the territories** (Missouri). **[consensus]**

## 5. Period terminology (1820 start)

Supplements parent §2.5/§2.6 terminology. **Anchor to the year 1820**, not the bare
party word.

- **Use:** Democratic-Republican / "Republican" / Jeffersonian (the BLUE party — the
  ancestor of today's *Democrats*, **not** the modern GOP); Federalist (genuinely
  still a regional party in 1820, dying nationally); "Old Republican" / "Quid"
  (strict-construction wing); "Era of Good Feelings"; the "American System"; strict
  vs. loose construction; the Second Bank of the United States; the Panic of 1819;
  the Missouri Compromise / the 36°30′ line; the Tallmadge Amendment; "universal
  white-male suffrage" / dropping property qualifications.
- **Use with care:** "**National Republican**" — *defensible as the game's RED label
  for a 1820→ campaign that's headed there*, but historically **premature in 1820**
  (the party is ~1825 at the earliest; the name ~1830). For strict real-history
  framing in 1820, the RED party is the **Federalists**; "National Republican" is
  correct only from ~1825 (Adams's men) / ~1830 (the name).
- **Avoid:** "**Democrat**" as a *party* name (the Democratic Party proper is born
  under Jackson ~1828 — a decade after this start); "**Whig**" (~1834); the **spoils
  system** as a Monroe-era feature (it's Jacksonian, post-1829); "**Manifest
  Destiny**" (coined 1845); the modern antislavery "**Republican**" (1854); and
  modern "liberal/conservative" — the live 1820 axes are **agrarian vs. commercial**
  and **strict vs. loose construction**, not the modern left/right. **Never** read
  the era's "Republican" as the GOP.
- **A game-token caveat:** the playtest narrates ideology bands as faction names
  ("Liberal Democrats," "Civil rights faction"). "**Civil rights**" as an ideology
  label (chunk-006 post 521: "the civil rights faction… +2 in all 1822 elections";
  chunk-011 post ~945: "You only need Lib or Civil Rights to ban slavery in a state")
  is a **modern anachronism** for 1820 — the period term for the relevant pole is
  **antislavery / Free-Soil-adjacent / "restrictionist"** (on slavery's *extension*),
  not "civil rights." [inference: the game uses "Civil Rights" as a cross-era ideology
  token; for 1820 framing, narrate it as antislavery sentiment.]

## 6. Common pop-history simplifications (1820-relevant)

Supplements parent §2.5/§2.6. The ones most likely to bite an 1820 start:

- **"The Era of Good Feelings was a calm, conflict-free consensus."** **[consensus]**
  It had no second *party*, but it was riven by the **Panic of 1819** and the
  **Missouri crisis (1820)** and seething personal rivalries that exploded in 1824.
  One-party did **not** mean harmonious — the 1820 start's whole premise is the
  cracks under the "good feelings" facade.
- **"Jefferson's Republicans are today's Republicans."** **[consensus]** False —
  opposite lineage; the BLUE Dem-Reps become the *Democrats*. The modern GOP is the
  *anti*-Jacksonian, antislavery party founded 30+ years later (1854).
- **"Andrew Jackson dominated this whole period."** **[consensus]** Not yet — in 1820
  Jackson is a military hero and minor political figure; his national rise is the
  1824/1828 elections, **after** where this playtest reached.
- **"Jacksonian 'democracy' meant democracy for everyone."** **[consensus, neutral]**
  The franchise expanded for **white men** (property qualifications dropped) while
  **race replaced property** as the bar (free Black men were disfranchised in many
  states in the same wave); slavery entrenched; women excluded throughout. State the
  franchise mechanic without modern editorializing.
- **"The slavery crisis started in the 1850s."** **[consensus]** It runs continuously
  from the **Missouri Compromise (1820)** — the very event the 1820 start centers —
  through 1850 to Kansas-Nebraska (1854). 1820 is the *opening* of the territorial
  fight, not a footnote to it. Jefferson's "fire bell in the night" reaction was to
  **Missouri (1820)**.
- **"The National Republicans / Whigs already existed in the early 1820s."**
  **[consensus]** No — in 1820 the opposition pole is the **Federalists** (dying);
  the Adams/Clay "National Republicans" coalesce only after 1824, and the Whigs after
  1833–34.

## 7. Game treatment vs. real history — divergence notes (for game-pm / game-master)

These are the highest-value notes for downstream era framing of the 1820 start.

- **Period-correct First-Party-System polarity at the START — and it's the *same*
  polarity as 1788/1800, not the 1856 flip.** The 1820 start opens with **BLUE =
  Democratic-Republicans** (the dominant party) and **RED = Federalists/National
  Republicans** (the dying/emerging opposition), with the agrarian/states'-rights/
  common-man pole on **BLUE** (chunk-001 post 1). This is faithful and matches parent
  §2.5. Do **not** import the 1856 RED=antislavery-progressive flip here.
- **THE 1820-SPECIFIC ANACHRONISM — "brand new National Republican Party (Red)" in
  1820.** The GA's opening calls RED "the brand new National Republican Party"
  (chunk-001 post 1), and players self-label "National Republicans" / "Marshall
  Federalists (National Republicans?)" (chunk-003). Historically there was **no
  National Republican Party in 1820** — that faction is ~1825 (Adams's men), and the
  *name* is ~1830 ([Wikipedia — National Republican Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Republican_Party)). In **1820** the RED party is the **Federalists** (genuinely still a regional party, dying nationally after the 1814–15 Hartford Convention). **Game-master framing:** for strict 1820 realism, RED = Federalists; the "National Republican" label is the game telescoping where the campaign is *headed* (an Adams/Clay nationalist party) into the start — defensible as a forward-look, but flag it as ~5–10 years early. This is the single most important divergence note for this start.
- **The persistent-Federalist-label problem is *milder* here than at later starts.**
  Parent §2.5/§2.6 flag that the 1800-playtest keeps RED = "Federalists" alive into
  the **1840s** (a real alt-history, since the party died ~1815–20). At a **1820**
  start the Federalist label is **era-plausible** (they still existed regionally in
  1820) — the divergence only becomes acute if this campaign, too, carries
  "Federalists" past ~1834 instead of becoming **Democrat vs. Whig**. Watch for it as
  the clock advances; it isn't yet a problem in the early 1820s the playtest reached.
- **Early alt-history personnel divergence — Van Buren dies in 1821.** The playtest's
  in-thread event kills **Martin Van Buren at 38 in July 1821** (chunk-004 post 272),
  removing the man who historically *builds the Democratic Party*. Combined with the
  slow pace (stalled ~1822–23), this campaign will **not** reproduce the real
  Jackson→Van Buren Democratic-Party-formation arc even if it resumes. **Game-master
  framing:** use the real Jacksonian chronology/figures for *texture*, but treat
  named officeholders and the *timing/occurrence* of set-piece events (party
  formation, Monroe Doctrine, the 1824 contingent election) as the campaign's own
  divergent timeline.
- **The game models the era's real issue axes well.** The opening foregrounds exactly
  the right 1820 tensions: the **Panic of 1819** and the **Second Bank**; the
  **internal-improvements/tariff vs. small-government** intra-party split; **white-male
  suffrage expansion** as a wedge ("extending the right to vote to every white male…
  no matter if they were land holders"); **Missouri / slavery in the territories**;
  and the coming **Monroe Doctrine** (chunk-001 post 1). The "Era of Good Feelings"
  is even modeled as a *destroyable* state ("Domestic Stability drops by -2, ending
  the Era of Good Feelings," chunk-006 post 521) — a nice mechanical analog to the
  historical erosion of one-party calm under economic/sectional stress.
- **"Era of Democracy" is the game's own band label, and it tracks a real phenomenon.**
  The name maps to the **expansion of white-male suffrage** and the rise of mass
  party politics (parent §2.6; §2 binding facts above). It is corroborated by real
  history as the era's defining democratic shift — just remember the franchise
  expanded for **white men** while **race replaced property** as the criterion.
- **Modern-token anachronism: "Civil Rights" as a 1820 ideology label.** The
  playtest's cross-era "Civil Rights" ideology token is used to gate **banning
  slavery in a state** (chunk-011) and to buff a "civil rights faction" in 1822
  elections (chunk-006). For 1820 framing, narrate this pole as **antislavery /
  restrictionist sentiment** (the Missouri-debate, Rufus-King kind), **not** the
  20th-century "civil rights movement." [inference]
- **Logistics caveat for digest-readers.** The first ~100 posts and many later ones
  are **spreadsheet/formula and draft-mechanics** chatter (career tracks, "the Master
  sheet looks broken," disgruntled-politician party-flips), not historical content.
  The historically load-bearing material is the **opening framing (chunk-001 post 1)**,
  the **faction/leader naming (chunk-003)**, the **in-thread newspaper events**
