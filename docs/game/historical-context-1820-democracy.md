## 2. Binding facts the 1820 start needs (treat as ground truth)

These supplement parent §2.5's binding facts (which already cover the Revolution of
1800, Louisiana Purchase, the embargo/War of 1812, and the Missouri Compromise).
**For the 1824→1840 binding facts the played arc needs, see §§9–11.**

- **The 1820 election — Monroe ran essentially unopposed and won 231–1.**
  **[consensus]** Monroe (Democratic-Republican) took **231 of 232 electoral votes**;
  the sole dissent was faithless elector **William Plumer of New Hampshire**, who
  voted for **John Quincy Adams**. The popular legend that Plumer dissented to
  preserve Washington's unique status as the only unanimous president is **false** —
  Plumer simply thought Monroe mediocre and Adams better. This is the *third and last*
  time a presidential candidate ran effectively unopposed. ([Britannica — 1820 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1820), [Annenberg Classroom — An Elector Changes His Vote](https://www.annenbergclassroom.org/timeline_event/an-elector-changes-his-vote/)) *(The 1820 start opens in the aftermath of exactly this election — "almost unanimously reelected… running virtually unopposed," chunk-001 post 1.)*
- **The 1816 election that broke the Federalists — Monroe beat Rufus King 183–34.**
  **[consensus]** Monroe/Tompkins (Dem-Rep) defeated the de facto Federalist King,
  whose party was in shambles after the **Dec 1814–Jan 1815 Hartford Convention** and
  the Treaty of Ghent. ([Britannica — 1816 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1816), [National Archives — 1816 Electoral College](https://www.archives.gov/electoral-college/1816)) *(The GA's opening post states King "lost 183-34 to Monroe, receiving only about 31% of the popular vote" and was "the first Presidential candidate to speak out against slavery, which cost him almost all his support in the South" — the electoral figures are correct; the antislavery-stance claim is a defensible characterization of King, who did oppose slavery's extension, though "first" is a stretch.)*
- **The Panic of 1819 was the first major U.S. financial depression.** **[consensus]**
  It struck two years into Monroe's presidency, during the "Era of Good Feelings."
  Causes: the post-Napoleonic-Wars collapse of global demand and commodity/agricultural
  prices, over-speculation in Western land, and — critically — the newly chartered
  **Second Bank of the United States** sharply **contracting credit** (calling in
  loans, demanding specie from state banks). Result: bank failures, foreclosures,
  urban unemployment, and a surge of popular resentment toward banks and speculators
  that **fractured the Democratic-Republicans over economic policy** and fed the
  democratic/anti-bank politics of the coming decade. Monroe could do little to
  blunt it. ([Wikipedia — Panic of 1819](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1819), [Economic Historian — Panic of 1819 as America's First Great Depression](https://www.economic-historian.com/p/panic-of-1819)) *(The opening post leans heavily on this — "deep and profound impact… first major financial crisis… weakened the dominant Democratic-Republican Party by exposing internal divisions over economic policy," chunk-001 post 1.)* **Note:** the man who "saved" the Second Bank through that brutal credit contraction was **Langdon Cheves** (Bank president 1819–22) — whom batch 11 elects **President Cheves** (§9, §12).
- **The Monroe Doctrine — December 2, 1823.** **[consensus]** First stated in
  Monroe's **seventh annual message to Congress (Dec 2, 1823)**. Three core
  principles: (1) **separate spheres** for the Americas and Europe; (2)
  **non-colonization**; (3) **non-intervention**. **Secretary of State John Quincy
  Adams** was the principal architect (he argued for a *unilateral* U.S. statement
  rather than a joint declaration with Britain). Crucially, **the U.S. lacked the
  military power to enforce it in 1823** — its teeth came from tacit alignment with
  the British Royal Navy.
  ([National Archives — Monroe Doctrine (1823)](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/monroe-doctrine), [State Dept Office of the Historian — Monroe Doctrine, 1823](https://history.state.gov/milestones/1801-1829/monroe)) *(Foreshadowed in chunk-001 post 1; had not fired in-game as of the early 1820s.)*
- **The "National Republican" label is anachronistic in 1820 — the party did not
  meaningfully exist until ~1825, and the name itself dates from ~1830.**
  **[consensus]** There was **no organized "National Republican Party" in 1820**. The
  faction grew out of the Democratic-Republicans who backed **John Quincy Adams** in
  the 1824 election (initially "Adams–Clay Republicans," 1825–29); the **name
  "National Republican" came into use around 1830**, after the 1828 loss to Jackson.
  They became the **Whigs ~1833–34**. ([Wikipedia — National Republican Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Republican_Party), [Britannica — National Republican Party](https://www.britannica.com/topic/National-Republican-Party)) *(In **1820** the RED party is the **dying Federalists**, not National Republicans. Note: batch 11 keeps using "National Republicans" / "Federalists" as RED labels right up to the **1840** convention — see §13.)*
- **The "Era of Democracy" name = the expansion of white-male suffrage.**
  **[consensus]** The period's defining democratic shift was the **dropping of
  property and taxpaying qualifications for voting**, extending the franchise to
  nearly all **adult white men** by ~1840. **Neutral framing:** the franchise
  expanded for **white men** while **race replaced property** as the criterion —
  several states simultaneously **stripped voting rights from free Black men**, and
  women remained disfranchised everywhere. ([Gilder Lehrman — Voting Rights and Restrictions in Pre-Emancipation America](https://www.gilderlehrman.org/history-resources/essays/voting-rights-and-restrictions-pre-emancipation-america), [National Humanities Center — The Expansion of Democracy during the Jacksonian Era](https://americainclass.org/the-expansion-of-democracy-during-the-jacksonian-era/)) *(The batch-11 game models this as a live legislative fight — a "**National Suffrage for White Male Property Owners**" amendment passes its committee 12–0 in the ~1834 Congress, chunk-024 post 1993.)*
- **The spoils system was Jacksonian (post-1828), NOT Monroe-era.** **[consensus]**
  Under the Virginia Dem-Rep presidents, **permanent tenure** was the de facto norm;
  J.Q. Adams refused to remove even opponents. The **"spoils system" / "rotation in
  office"** is a **Jackson administration** development: on taking office in **1829**,
  Jackson dismissed ~**900 federal officeholders (~10%)** for partisan allies. The
  phrase **"to the victor belong the spoils"** comes from Sen. **William L. Marcy in
  1832**. ([Britannica — Spoils system](https://www.britannica.com/topic/spoils-system), [Miller Center — Jackson: Domestic Affairs](https://millercenter.org/president/jackson/domestic-affairs)) *(At the 1820 start the patronage norm is still Virginia-dynasty permanent tenure; spoils belongs to the post-1828 arc the batch-11 game reached.)*

## 4. Era-appropriate factions / ideologies / interests (1820 lens)

The story is **one dominant party (Dem-Reps) beginning to fragment** under economic
(Panic of 1819) and sectional (Missouri) stress — *before* the clean Democrat/Whig
split of the next decade. The playtests' **B1–B5 / R1–R5** structure maps onto this:

- **BLUE = Democratic-Republicans** (dominant). Split into a **"Liberal"**
  internal-improvements/tariff/Second-Bank wing (historically → National Republicans
  → Whigs) and an **"RW Pop / Trad" Jeffersonian** small-government, strict-construction,
  suffrage-expansion wing (historically → core of the Jacksonian Democrats).
- **RED = the dying Federalists** (a New England/Northeast rump) — and, per the GA's
  framing, the nascent "National Republican" pole. *Historically the RED party in 1820
  is genuinely the Federalists; the National Republican name is ~5–10 years premature.*
- **Live interests** the era turned on: **Western farmers/settlers** (cheap land,
  expansion), **Southern planters** (slavery, low tariff), **Northern
  merchants/manufacturers** (protective tariff after 1816), the **Second Bank** as a
  political lightning rod, and the **new sectional fault line over slavery in the
  territories** (Missouri). **[consensus]** *(How this evolves over the played
  1828→1840 arc — and how/whether it tracks the real Democrat-vs-Whig split — is
  §13.)*

## 5. Period terminology (1820 start)

**Anchor to the year 1820**, not the bare party word. **For 1828→1856 terminology
the played arc needs (Whig, Locofoco, "pet banks," Manifest Destiny, Free-Soil),
see §14.**

- **Use:** Democratic-Republican / "Republican" / Jeffersonian (the BLUE party — the
  ancestor of today's *Democrats*, **not** the modern GOP); Federalist (genuinely
  still a regional party in 1820, dying nationally); "Old Republican" / "Quid"
  (strict-construction wing); "Era of Good Feelings"; the "American System"; strict
  vs. loose construction; the Second Bank; the Panic of 1819; the Missouri Compromise
  / the 36°30′ line; "universal white-male suffrage" / dropping property qualifications.
- **Use with care:** "**National Republican**" — *defensible as the game's RED label
  for a 1820→ campaign headed there*, but historically **premature in 1820** (party
  ~1825; name ~1830). For strict 1820 realism the RED party is the **Federalists**.
- **Avoid (at the 1820 start):** "**Democrat**" as a *party* name (born under Jackson
  ~1828); "**Whig**" (~1834); the **spoils system** as a Monroe-era feature; "**Manifest
  Destiny**" (coined 1845); the modern antislavery "**Republican**" (1854); and modern
  "liberal/conservative." The live 1820 axes are **agrarian vs. commercial** and
  **strict vs. loose construction**. **Never** read the era's "Republican" as the GOP.
- **A game-token caveat:** the playtests narrate ideology bands as faction names
  ("Liberal Democrats," "Civil Rights faction"). "**Civil Rights**" as an ideology
  label is a **modern anachronism** for 1820–40 — the period term for the relevant
  pole is **antislavery / Free-Soil-adjacent / "restrictionist"** (on slavery's
  *extension*). [inference]

## 6. Common pop-history simplifications (1820-relevant)

The ones most likely to bite an 1820 start (those most relevant to the *played*
1824→1840 arc are in **§15**):

- **"The Era of Good Feelings was a calm, conflict-free consensus."** **[consensus]**
  It had no second *party*, but it was riven by the **Panic of 1819** and the
  **Missouri crisis (1820)**. One-party did **not** mean harmonious.
- **"Jefferson's Republicans are today's Republicans."** **[consensus]** False —
  opposite lineage; the BLUE Dem-Reps become the *Democrats*. The modern GOP is the
  *anti*-Jacksonian, antislavery party founded 30+ years later (1854).
- **"Andrew Jackson dominated this whole period."** **[consensus]** Not in 1820 — his
  national rise is the 1824/1828 elections. *(In batch 11 he never rises at all; the
  Blue presidency goes to Benton, not Jackson — §13.)*
- **"Jacksonian 'democracy' meant democracy for everyone."** **[consensus, neutral]**
  Franchise expanded for **white men**; **race replaced property** as the bar.
- **"The slavery crisis started in the 1850s."** **[consensus]** It runs continuously
  from the **Missouri Compromise (1820)**. *(Batch 11 surfaces "Abolish Slavery"
  amendments as recurring CRISIS bills as early as the ~1834 Congress — §11.)*

## 9. Binding facts for the played 1824→1840 arc (treat as ground truth)

- **Election of 1824 — no electoral majority; House contingent election; the "Corrupt
  Bargain."** **[consensus]** Four Democratic-Republicans split the field. Electoral
  votes: **Jackson 99, J.Q. Adams 84, Crawford 41, Clay 37** (131 needed). Under the
  **12th Amendment** the House chose among the **top three** (so Clay, who came 4th,
  was excluded), each state delegation casting **one vote**. Speaker **Clay threw his
  support to Adams**, who won on the **first ballot, 13 states, February 9, 1825**.
  Adams then named **Clay Secretary of State** — the post seen as the stepping-stone
  to the presidency — and Jacksonians branded it the **"Corrupt Bargain,"** which
  poisoned Adams's term and fueled Jackson's 1828 landslide. ([National Archives — The 1824 Election and the "Corrupt Bargain"](https://prologue.blogs.archives.gov/2020/10/22/the-1824-presidential-election-and-the-corrupt-bargain/), [Britannica — 1824 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1824), [Miller Center — Corrupt Bargain](https://millercenter.org/contested-presidential-elections/corrupt-bargain)) *(The batch-11 game's 1824 cycle plays out as an in-game election with a "President pro tempore" race etc., not a real contingent election — and its winner is the fictional President Benton, not Adams/Jackson, see §13.)*
- **Election of 1828 — Jackson's revenge; birth of the Democratic Party; spoils.**
  **[consensus]** Jackson (now leading the new **Democratic Party**) crushed Adams,
  inaugurating the **Second Party System** (Democrats vs. National Republicans →
  Whigs). On taking office (1829) Jackson institutionalized **"rotation in office."**
  ([Miller Center — Jackson: Campaigns and Elections](https://millercenter.org/president/jackson/campaigns-and-elections), [Britannica — Spoils system](https://www.britannica.com/topic/spoils-system)) *(Batch 11 re-elects President **Benton** in a landslide in 1828 — "four consecutive two-term presidents, following Jefferson, Adams, and Monroe," chunk-015 post 1425 — so Jackson never takes the White House in this save, see §13.)*
- **Nullification Crisis (1832–33) — Force Bill + Compromise Tariff.** **[consensus]**
  South Carolina, led ideologically by **John C. Calhoun** ("South Carolina
  Exposition," doctrine of nullification), adopted the **Ordinance of Nullification
  (Nov 24, 1832)** declaring the Tariffs of 1828 ("Tariff of Abominations") and 1832
  **null and void** and threatening secession. **Jackson** answered with the
  **Nullification Proclamation (Dec 10, 1832)** asserting federal supremacy. On
  **March 1, 1833** Congress passed **both** the **Force Bill** (authorizing military
  collection of tariffs) and **Henry Clay's Compromise Tariff of 1833** (gradual
  10-year reduction). South Carolina rescinded the tariff ordinance — then defiantly
  nullified the Force Bill. ([Britannica — Nullification Crisis](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Nullification-Crisis), [Britannica — Force Bill](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Force-Bill), [Britannica — Compromise of 1833](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Compromise-of-1833)) *(Batch 11 surfaces the tariff/federal-supremacy fight directly: in its ~1834 Congress a senator proposes "**Expand Presidential Power to Allow the President to Force States to Comply with Federal Revenue Laws**" — a near-literal Force Bill — alongside competing 10%/40% tariff bills, chunk-024 posts 1965–1982.)*
- **Bank War (1832–36) — veto, pet banks, Specie Circular.** **[consensus]** Jackson
  **vetoed the Second Bank's recharter on July 10, 1832** (constitutional + populist
  "privilege to the wealthy few" grounds), making the Bank the central 1832 campaign
  issue. After re-election he **removed federal deposits** into ~**89 state "pet
  banks"** (1833); Bank president **Nicholas Biddle** retaliated by contracting
  credit. The **Specie Circular (July 11, 1836)** required **gold/silver for public-land
  purchases**, helping trigger the Panic of 1837. ([Miller Center — Bank Veto, July 10 1832](https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/presidential-speeches/july-10-1832-bank-veto), [Miller Center — The Bank War](https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/educational-resources/bank-war), [Britannica — Specie Circular](https://www.britannica.com/event/Specie-Circular)) *(The Bank is a live, recurring CRISIS bill in batch 11: "Establish Bank of the United States" gets filibustered, returns, and is ultimately **replaced by an "independent treasury"** under President Dudley c. 1838–40, chunk-024 post 1963 / chunk-033 posts 2527–2529 — exactly the real-history Bank → Independent Treasury arc, see §11.)*
- **Indian Removal Act (1830) / Trail of Tears (1838–39).** **[consensus]** Jackson
  signed the **Indian Removal Act on May 28, 1830**, authorizing land exchanges west
  of the Mississippi. In **Worcester v. Georgia (March 3, 1832)** the Marshall Court
  held (5–1) that Georgia could not impose its laws on Cherokee land; **Georgia and
  Jackson ignored it.** Forced removal of the Cherokee (the **Trail of Tears**, fall
  1838–39 under Van Buren) killed ~**4,000 of ~16,000**. ([National Archives — Jackson's Message on Indian Removal (1830)](https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/jacksons-message-to-congress-on-indian-removal), [Britannica — Worcester v. Georgia](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Worcester-v-Georgia), [Britannica — Trail of Tears](https://www.britannica.com/event/Trail-of-Tears))
- **Panic of 1837 + Independent Treasury.** **[consensus]** Banks suspended specie
  payments (May 1837); a British downturn cut cotton prices and loans; a multi-year
  depression followed. **Van Buren's** answer was the **Independent ("Sub-")Treasury**
  — "divorcing" federal funds from banks — finally passed as the **Independent
  Treasury Act of 1840**. ([Britannica — Panic of 1837](https://www.britannica.com/event/Panic-of-1837), [Miller Center — Van Buren: Domestic Affairs](https://millercenter.org/president/vanburen/domestic-affairs)) *(Batch 11 plays a literal Economic-Stability "CRISIS" with the same resolution — see §11.)*
- **Election of 1840 — "Tippecanoe and Tyler Too"; Whig log-cabin campaign; Tyler
  succession.** **[consensus]** Whig **William Henry Harrison** beat Van Buren
  **234–60** in the first modern mass-marketing campaign (log cabins, hard cider,
  "Tippecanoe and Tyler too"). Harrison **died after one month (April 1841)**; **John
  Tyler** asserted full presidential powers (the "**Tyler Precedent**" for succession,
  later 25th Amendment). ([Britannica — 1840 election](https://www.britannica.com/event/United-States-presidential-election-of-1840), [Miller Center — Harrison: Campaigns and Elections](https://millercenter.org/president/harrison/campaigns-and-elections), [Miller Center — Tyler: Life in Brief](https://millercenter.org/president/tyler/life-in-brief)) *(Batch 11 reached the **1840 conventions** — "1840 National Republican Convention, Albany NY" / "1840 Democratic-Republican Convention, Winston-Salem NC," chunk-032 post 2462 — with no Harrison; its incumbent is the fictional President Dudley, see §13.)*

## 10. The Manifest Destiny bridge (1840–1856) — what comes next

The game's "**Manifest Destiny (1840–1856)**" band is the late-Jacksonian / Whig-vs-
Democrat decade. Batch 11 reached only its **threshold (the 1840 cycle)**, so these
are framing for where a continuing campaign heads — and they bridge into the 1856
antebellum scenario the game already documents. **[consensus] throughout:**

- **Texas annexation (1845)** by joint resolution under Tyler/Polk; **"Manifest
  Destiny"** itself was a phrase **coined in 1845** (John L. O'Sullivan) and was
  **contested even at the time** (Whigs largely opposed expansion as a slave-power
  project).
- **Mexican–American War (April 1846 – Feb 1848)**, sparked by the Texas border
  dispute (Nueces vs. Rio Grande); ended by the **Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (Feb 2,
  1848)** ceding the present-day Southwest + California (the **Mexican Cession**).
- **Wilmot Proviso (Aug 8, 1846)** — proposed banning slavery in any territory won
  from Mexico; repeatedly blocked in the Senate but **reframed national politics
  around slavery in the territories** and seeded the **Free-Soil** movement.
- **Compromise of 1850** (CA admitted free; territories on popular sovereignty;
  stronger Fugitive Slave Act; Texas boundary settled) — a five-part package largely
  shepherded by **Clay, Webster, and Douglas**.
- **Kansas–Nebraska Act (1854)** — **Douglas's** popular-sovereignty bill that
  **repealed the Missouri Compromise's 36°30′ line**, ignited "Bleeding Kansas,"
  shattered the Whigs, and **birthed the modern (antislavery) Republican Party**
  — the direct on-ramp to the game's 1856 scenario (parent §3).
- ([State Dept — Annexation of Texas / Mexican War](https://history.state.gov/milestones/1830-1860/texas-annexation), [Britannica — Mexican-American War](https://www.britannica.com/event/Mexican-American-War), [Senate — Kansas-Nebraska Act](https://www.senate.gov/artandhistory/history/minute/Kansas_Nebraska_Act.htm))

## 12. Key figures the played arc leans on (real-history capsule)

The campaign uses **real people** in its presidencies and leadership. Capsules so the
game-master can keep them era-accurate — and see how far the game's use departs (§13):

- **Thomas Hart Benton** (1782–1858) — **Missouri's first U.S. senator, 1821–1851**;
  Democratic-Republican → **Jacksonian Democrat**, Jackson's **chief Senate ally**.
  Nicknamed **"Old Bullion"** for his **hard-money** crusade (against paper money and
  the Second Bank); a champion of **cheap Western land / expansion**; engineered the
  1837 "expunging" of Jackson's censure; later broke with the slave-power and lost his
  seat over it (1851). **Never president or vice president.** ([Senate — Thomas Hart Benton, Featured Biography](https://www.senate.gov/senators/FeaturedBios/Featured_Bio_Benton.htm), [House — BENTON, Thomas Hart](https://history.house.gov/People/Detail/9291)) *(Batch 11 makes him its **first** Blue **President** — see §13.)*

  **president of the Second Bank of the United States 1819–22**, credited with
  **"saving" the Bank** through brutal credit contraction (the "Hercules of the
  Bank"). A states'-rights man who sympathized with nullification's *ends* but not its

- **Daniel Webster** (1782–1852) — MA; the great **Whig** nationalist orator ("Liberty

## 13. Game treatment vs. real history — divergence notes for the played arc (HIGH VALUE)

**These are the most important context notes for the PM / game-master from batch 11.**
This campaign keeps the **First-Party-System frame frozen for two extra decades** and
fills the presidency with **fictional Democratic-Republicans**:

1. **The party system NEVER splits into Whigs vs. Democrats.** Through **1840**, the
   game still runs **"Democratic-Republicans" (BLUE) vs. "National
   Republicans"/"Federalists" (RED)** — the 1824-era frame — with faction nicknames
   like "Marshall Federalists," "Arch-Federalists," "Scott Federalists," "King
   National-Republicans," and "Popular Sovereignty Democrats" (chunks 8, 16, 21, 32).
   In **real** history the Democratic Party formed under Jackson (~1828) and the
   **Whigs** (~1834) replaced the National Republicans as the opposition. **The
   batch-11 timeline has no Jackson presidency, no Whig Party, and no Democratic
   Party-formation arc** — Lars even jokes "It's a pity the Democratic-Republicans
   didn't disintegrate last election like in real life" (chunk-008 post 726).
   **Game-master framing:** use real Whig-vs-Democrat substance (tariff, Bank,
   internal improvements, expansion, slavery) for *texture*, but treat the **party
   names and the absence of the 1828/1834 realignment** as this save's own divergent
   politics. RED ≠ Whigs and BLUE ≠ the modern Democrats here; the polarity is still
   the frozen 1824 frame.
2. **An unbroken line of FICTIONAL Democratic-Republican presidents.** The Blue party
   wins every presidency the campaign played, with figures who **never actually held
   the office**:
   - **President Thomas Hart Benton** — Blue president from the early arc, **re-elected
     1828** in a landslide (chunk-015 post 1425). Real Benton was a *senator* who never
     ran for president. The game even frames him as the heir to "Jefferson, Adams, and
     Monroe" — i.e. it slots him where real history has **J.Q. Adams then Jackson**.
   - **President Langdon Cheves** — wins **1832** in a near-sweep (carried virtually
     every state, chunk-021 post 1778), **re-elected 1836**. Real Cheves was a Bank
     president/Speaker, never president.
   - **President Enoch Lincoln** — Cheves's **Vice President**, succeeds to the
     presidency when **Cheves is assassinated mid-term** (~1834–36; "President Langdon
     Cheves dies 2/50… Vice President Enoch Lincoln now assumes the presidency… in
     accordance with the 13th Amendment," chunk-027 post 276). **This is Enoch Lincoln
     of Maine — NOT Abraham Lincoln.** (The game's "13th Amendment" here is an in-game
     presidential-succession rule, not the real 1865 abolition amendment — another
     anachronistic-token caution.)
   - **President Dudley** (Edward Bishop Dudley) — the Blue incumbent by the **1840**
     cycle, signing the Independent-Treasury bill (chunk-033 posts 2527–2529).
   - **Other fictional officeholders of note:** **DeWitt Clinton** as a long-serving
     **Vice President**; **Henry Clay** as a **BLUE** Vice-Presidential/faction figure
     (opposite his real anti-Jackson Whig path); **Winfield Scott** and **Daniel
     Webster** as **RED** Party Leaders. **Game-master framing:** treat all
     president/VP identities and the *timing* of successions/assassinations as the
     campaign's own alternate timeline; do **not** reconcile them to the real
     Adams→Jackson→Van Buren→Harrison→Tyler sequence.
3. **The real *issues* are modeled with surprising fidelity — keep these accurate.**
   Despite the fictional cast, the campaign exercises the genuine Jacksonian agenda:
   a recurring **Second Bank** CRISIS bill that is ultimately **replaced by an
   "independent treasury"** (the real Bank-War → 1840 Independent-Treasury arc); a
   near-literal **Force Bill** ("force states to comply with federal revenue laws")
   and **dueling 10%/40% tariff** bills (the Nullification/tariff fight); a
   **white-male-property-suffrage** amendment; recurring **"Abolish Slavery"** CRISIS
   amendments that always fail (the real antebellum trajectory); and an active
   **Economic-Stability crisis** (a Panic-of-1837 analog). The game-master can lean on
   the **real** Bank War / nullification / Panic / removal history (§9) to narrate
   these beats faithfully even though the people pulling the levers are invented.
4. **"Manifest Destiny" is named but barely reached.** The campaign hit the **1840
   conventions** and stopped; the **Texas/Mexican-War/Compromise-of-1850/Kansas-Nebraska**
   content of the game's 1840–56 band (§10) is **framing for a continuation**, not
   events this save played. (The thread itself notes the Mexico war is an in-game
   "Manifest Destiny" event for "the 1840s," `152c2881` chunk-004 post 625.)
5. **Anachronistic tokens to watch (carry-overs from §5/§7).** "Civil Rights" as an
   ideology card gating slavery bills (narrate as **antislavery/restrictionist**); the
   in-game "13th Amendment" = a **succession** rule, not abolition; modern interest
   cards ("Big Corporations," "Military-Industrial," "Globalists," "Wall Street,"
   "LW Media") appear on 1820s–40s faction sheets (chunk-008 post 729) and are
   **cross-era game tokens**, not period interest groups — for era flavor, map them to
   period equivalents (manufacturers/merchants, the "war hawks"/army contractors,
   internationalist merchants, the Second-Bank/finance interest, the partisan press).

## 15. Pop-history simplifications for the played arc (supplements §6)

- **"The Corrupt Bargain was proven corruption."** **[consensus]** No bribery was ever
  proven; Clay backing Adams (a fellow nationalist) over Jackson was ordinary politics
  — but the *appearance* (Clay → Secretary of State) was politically fatal.
- **"Jackson destroyed the Bank because it was corrupt."** **[contested/consensus]** The
  Bank War was as much about **executive power and Jackson's populist politics** as about
  the Bank's conduct; historians debate whether killing the Bank **worsened** the
  1837 panic. Present it as a genuine policy/power struggle, not a morality play.
- **"Worcester v. Georgia stopped Indian removal."** **[consensus]** The opposite — the
  Court ruled for the Cherokee but **Jackson and Georgia ignored it**, and removal
  proceeded (Trail of Tears, 1838–39). (Jackson's quoted "John Marshall has made his
  decision; now let him enforce it" is **apocryphal** — flag if it comes up.)
- **"Manifest Destiny was a national consensus."** **[consensus]** It was **contested
  from the start** — Whigs and antislavery Northerners attacked expansion (especially the
  Mexican War) as a **slave-power land grab**.
- **"The Whigs and Democrats only differed on style."** **[consensus]** They split on
  real things: the **Bank/credit/Independent Treasury**, the **tariff**, **federal
  internal improvements**, and **executive power** ("King Andrew"). The batch-11 game's
  *issues* capture this even though it keeps the older party *names* (§13).
